1. CODEACADEMY

This one is my favorite. The programming lessons are free and when you visit the website, you are immediately hooked. The design is super-minimalist and the interface very easy to follow. Whatever you click, you are seamlessly guided through small chunks of exercises.

[ad]

Codeacademy was launched in 2011 and has so far attracted more than 20 millions students who come to learn HTML, CSS, jQuery, JavaScript, PHP, Python, Ruby, or practice by developing a specific project.

2. TREEHOUSE

The website hosts a library of educational videos to help you learn web design, web development, and iOS development. Try not to get yourself overwhelmed and rather choose one of the pre-arranged tracks (e.g. Front-end, Android, WordPress…). Once you start the track, you will see a list of courses to follow step by step. The courses include quizzes and interactive code challenges to practice the newly acquired skills.

Treehouse offers basic ($25/month) and pro ($49/month) plans, with 14-days free trials. The latter includes extra talks, interviews, and workshops.

3. ONE MONTH RAILS

If you're in a rush and need to build your first web application fast (within 30 days), try this series of video lessons and tutorials designed for total beginners. The cost is $99 (8 hours of course videos, unlimited access for a year) and you will get the hang of Ruby on Rails, HTML, CSS, JavaScript, Git, Github, Heroku, and Bootstrap.

4. DASH

Again, if you mainly wish to learn HTML, CSS, or JavaScript, and start creating your own websites today, Dash is a place to go. You will learn the basics in a very interactive way by completing 5 projects (e.g. build a responsive blog theme) and unlocking 82 skills step by step.

It's free and all you need to do is to sign up with Twitter, Tumblr, or email.

5. UDEMY

Udemy is an educational marketplace offering a big variety of online courses. Individuals or institutions can create courses and upload materials on virtually any topic (both free or paid) there. The most popular in the technology field include Java for Complete Beginners, Learn HTML5 Programming From Scratch, MySQL Database For Beginners, Coding for Entrepreneurs Basic, or Creating iPhone & iPad Apps Training.

You can search relevant classes by specific subject or overall rating based on student reviews. The big advantage is a community of fellow students with whom you can discuss your progress or hurdles.

6. HarvardX — INTRODUCTION TO COMPUTER SCIENCE

The campus version of this online course is one of the most popular at Harvard College. CS50x is an introduction to the world of computer science and programming. It covers numerous topics (abstraction, algorithms, data structures, encapsulation, resource management, security, software engineering, and web development) and languages (C, PHP, and JavaScript plus SQL, CSS, and HTML).

You can follow the lessons and assignments according to your own pace. If you score high enough on problem sets, you will receive a certificate of completion. The course is free but to aim for a verified certificate you need to pay a fee ($90). You can even enroll in the traditional, semester-long course ($2050) to earn a Harvard credit.

Like Udemy, this platform offers a range of online courses. Coursera partnered with top universities and institutions (e.g. Princeton University, Columbia University, National Geographic Society, MoMA and many others) to provide everybody with access to quality education.

Each course contains several video lectures. Students are also presented with a few assignments (optional unless you want to obtain a certificate), and they can participate in the course forums.

8. KHAN ACADEMY

Khan Academy is a renowned non-profit organization whose online platform offers an enormous library of content, so that anybody can educate himself on practically any conceivable topic (in the past, it received grants from Google and the Bill and Melinda Gates Foundation). The tutorials in the computer science section start from the very basics (e.g. how to draw, color, outline shapes) and consist of videos, coding walk-throughs, challenges, and projects.

All is very interactive and fun. You can post comments, ask questions, and share feedback at every stage. You have also access to programs other people made, and you can develop their spin-offs. When you create your own first programs (JavaScript), you are given snippets of code to use them as cues.

9. UDACITY

Compared to Coursera, programming and technology courses offered by Udacity have no start/finish dates. It gives you more freedom to choose your own pace, however you aren't put under pressure (often necessary) to systematically follow the chosen track

[youtube https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=4z37fUbpJ3s]

Once you pick the course of your liking, you can either view “free courseware” (information, lectures, auto-graded exercises) or “enrol in the course” (personal couch, code review, project guidance, verified certificate) for a fee. Udacity will also soon offer nanodegrees, i.e. a new kind of online curriculum developed jointly with major tech industry leaders to prepare you better for your future job (Data Analyst, Front-End Web Developer, Back-End Web Developer, iOS developer).

10. GIBBON

I bet you often feel lost in the vast amount of resources available on the web. Maybe you're lucky to have many well-read friends who will send you a list of articles, blog posts, or videos on the topic of your interest when you decide to delve into something new. But most people don't and internet forums are full of “Where do I start if I want to learn…” questions. That's when peer-to-peer curation comes to stage.

Gibbon is a newborn child of a Dutch startup which strives to take a bit different approach to self-education. Users can follow learning flows, i.e. collections of selected content, put together by other users. The web app will make playlists of individual chapters for you, which you can then mark as read, or skip in case you are not in the mood. The most popular flows focus on design, marketing, and programming.

11. CODEMENTOR

As it is the case with anything you learn, the more you know the more obstacles come into your way. Reading another article, or browsing another forum is often just not enough.

Codementor will provide you with one-on-one help by connecting you with more experienced coders via screen sharing, video, and text chat. After posting your request, you will see a list of experts together with their short bio and rate (per 15-min session). You can connect with the mentor of your choice instantly or schedule for later. The rates start around $5-10 and you will get a full refund if you are not satisfied.
